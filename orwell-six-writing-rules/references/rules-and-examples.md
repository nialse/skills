# Orwell Six Writing Rules: Source Map and Examples

## Authority Order

1. O1 is the primary authority for Orwell's intent, rule order, and warnings about stale imagery, vague abstractions, padded verbs, passive voice, and jargon.
2. P1-P6 provide modern plain-language examples, especially for audience, active voice, hidden verbs, short sentences, simple words, and jargon.
3. The user's audience, requested tone, and factual constraints outrank mechanical edits.

The rule wordings below are paraphrases of Orwell's six rules.

## R1. Use fresh images or no image. Sources: O1

Check for familiar figures of speech that pretend to clarify but only signal attitude. Mixed metaphors are strong evidence that the writer is not seeing the image.

BAD:

```text
This initiative is a silver bullet that will move the needle and keep us ahead of the curve.
```

GOOD:

```text
This initiative should reduce review time and help us respond before competitors do.
```

GOOD, when an image earns its place:

```text
The policy reads like a map with the road names rubbed off.
```

COUNTEREXAMPLE:

```text
The project reached a milestone.
```

Do not rewrite ordinary dead metaphors that now behave like plain words unless they draw attention to themselves or blur the meaning.

## R2. Use the short word when it means the same thing. Sources: O1, P4

Prefer familiar words over inflated ones when accuracy does not change. Test by asking whether the shorter word loses meaning, register, or needed precision.

BAD:

```text
We will utilize the platform to facilitate collaboration across departments.
```

GOOD:

```text
We will use the platform to help departments work together.
```

COUNTEREXAMPLE:

```text
The patient has hypertension.
```

Do not replace a precise technical or medical term with a vague short word. Define it if the audience may not know it.

## R3. Cut words that do no work. Sources: O1, P3, P6

Remove padding, repeated meaning, throat-clearing, hidden verbs, and long setup phrases. Keep words that carry legal force, timing, uncertainty, politeness, rhythm, or voice.

BAD:

```text
At this point in time, we are in the process of conducting a review of the policy.
```

GOOD:

```text
We are reviewing the policy.
```

BAD:

```text
The team made a decision to perform an analysis of the logs.
```

GOOD:

```text
The team decided to analyze the logs.
```

COUNTEREXAMPLE:

```text
You must file the appeal within 30 days after receiving the decision.
```

Do not cut time limits, scope words, exceptions, or conditions that change obligations.

## R4. Use active voice when the actor matters. Sources: O1, P2

Prefer active voice when readers need to know who did, must do, or will do something. Passive voice can be clearer when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, legally implied, or less important than the thing acted on.

BAD:

```text
The report was reviewed by Mara.
```

GOOD:

```text
Mara reviewed the report.
```

BAD:

```text
Mistakes were made during deployment.
```

GOOD:

```text
We made mistakes during deployment.
```

COUNTEREXAMPLE:

```text
The samples were contaminated before delivery.
```

If the actor is unknown and the contamination is the point, passive voice is useful.

COUNTEREXAMPLE:

```text
Your lease will be terminated if you do not pay.
```

If the law or contract supplies the actor and the consequence matters most, passive voice may be the cleanest form.

## R5. Use everyday English unless the specialist term is necessary. Sources: O1, P1, P4, P5

Replace jargon, imported phrases, abstract labels, and scientific words when an everyday phrase is accurate for the reader. Keep necessary terms of art, then define them briefly when the audience needs help.

BAD:

```text
The team operationalized learnings via a post hoc analysis.
```

GOOD:

```text
The team learned from reviewing the data afterward.
```

BAD:

```text
The committee will commence stakeholder engagement prior to implementation.
```

GOOD:

```text
The committee will talk with stakeholders before it starts.
```

COUNTEREXAMPLE:

```text
Use OAuth 2.0 for authorization.
```

Do not replace a standard technical protocol name with a homemade paraphrase.

## R6. Break the rule before writing something false, ugly, or childish. Sources: O1, P2, P5

Use this rule as the final quality gate. If a rule-driven edit sounds worse, hides a needed distinction, or insults the reader's intelligence, keep the better sentence and explain why if asked.

BAD mechanical rewrite:

```text
The cancer test found a bad growth.
```

GOOD:

```text
The biopsy found a malignant tumor.
```

BAD mechanical rewrite:

```text
The court made him not guilty.
```

GOOD:

```text
The court acquitted him.
```

COUNTEREXAMPLE:

```text
The vaccine was approved after a randomized trial.
```

This sentence uses passive voice and specialist wording, but it is accurate, compact, and natural for a public-health context.

## Review Checklist

R1: Does every image clarify something, or is it stale decoration?

R2: Can a shorter word replace a longer one without losing meaning?

R3: Can any word or phrase be removed with no loss?

R4: Does the sentence name the actor when the actor matters?

R5: Can jargon be replaced with everyday English for this audience?

R6: Did the rewrite preserve truth, voice, register, rhythm, and necessary precision?
