# Ad Copy Writing Test

Use this skill to make writing easier to see, check, and own. Keep the writer's intent, voice, and level of formality intact unless the user asks for a stronger change.

For source status and attribution notes, read `references/attribution.md` only when the user asks where the framework or examples came from.

## Workflow

1. Split the text into sentences or claim units. Treat headlines, taglines, fragments, and bullets as units.
2. For each unit, ask three questions:
   - Can I visualize it?
   - Can I falsify it?
   - Can nobody else say this?
3. Mark each answer as yes, mostly, or no.
4. Diagnose the pattern:
   - Three no answers means the unit is probably vague, generic, or meaningless.
   - Two no answers means the unit needs revision unless it is intentionally transitional.
   - Three yes answers means the unit is probably concrete, credible, and distinctive.
5. Rewrite weak units by adding observable details, checkable claims, and subject-specific material.
6. Do not invent facts. If proof is missing, either keep the claim softer, ask for the missing fact, or use a clearly marked placeholder.

## The Three Checks

### Can I visualize it?

The reader should be able to picture a person, object, place, action, behavior, scene, comparison, or measurable state.

Weak signals:

- abstract nouns without evidence, such as transformation, innovation, excellence, impact, or change
- verbs that do not show action, such as empower, enable, drive, unlock, or revolutionize
- category claims without a scene or mechanism

Revision moves:

- replace abstractions with concrete nouns and verbs
- name the actor, audience, setting, object, or behavior
- add a visible before-and-after state when supported

### Can I falsify it?

The claim should be specific enough that it could be checked, disproven, counted, compared, observed, or meaningfully challenged.

Weak signals:

- unverifiable scale claims, such as industry-changing, best-in-class, world-class, or seamless
- aspirations presented as facts
- claims with no metric, mechanism, timeframe, scope, source, or observed behavior

Revision moves:

- add a number, date, scope, source, mechanism, constraint, or observable outcome
- convert broad promises into testable claims
- separate opinion, ambition, and verified fact

### Can nobody else say this?

The line should become weaker, false, or less natural if the name of the person, brand, product, or situation is removed.

Weak signals:

- a competitor or unrelated person could say the same sentence unchanged
- the sentence describes the category instead of the subject
- the line depends on tone instead of substance

Revision moves:

- add the subject's specific audience, history, behavior, proof, tradeoff, geography, constraint, material, or mechanism
- include a contrast that fits this subject and not the whole category
- keep useful oddness when it is true and clarifying

## Output Shape

When revising, put the revised version first. Then add only the shortest useful diagnostic, focused on what changed and why.

When auditing, use a compact table:

| Unit | Visual | Falsifiable | Distinctive | Fix |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Use yes, mostly, or no in the three middle columns.

When facts are missing, mark the issue as `needs proof` and do not over-specify the rewrite.

## Examples

Weak:

> "Don't just get a job, change an entire industry."

Evaluation: no visual scene, no falsifiable claim, and no distinctive owner. Almost any ambitious employer could say it.

Strong:

> "Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio."

Evaluation: visual, checkable, and unusually specific to New Balance's cross-demographic appeal.

## Guardrails

- Preserve the user's tone and cadence where possible.
- Favor concrete truth over cleverness.
- Use specificity only when it is supported by the supplied text, common context, or cited research.
- Do not turn every sentence into a statistic. Scenes, named audiences, and observable behavior can also make writing falsifiable.
- Do not remove necessary connective tissue just because it is less vivid than a claim sentence.
